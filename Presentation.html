Introduction to Vector-Borne Diseases in India

Global Burden

  • >1 billion people affected by vector-borne diseases annually
  • Arboviruses: RNA viruses transmitted by arthropod vectors
  • Mosquitoes: Primary vectors for JE and Chikungunya
  • Climate change: Expanding vector habitats and transmission seasons

Indian Perspective

  • NVBDCP coordinates control of 6 major vector-borne diseases
  • IDSP provides real-time surveillance and outbreak response
  • Monsoon season: Peak transmission period (July-October)
  • Urban vs Rural: Different epidemiological patterns

Why JE and Chikungunya Matter for MBBS Students

  • Clinical relevance: Common in community medicine rotations
  • Public health impact: Significant burden on Indian healthcare system
  • Prevention focus: Community-based interventions and vector control
  • Policy involvement: Understanding NVBDCP and UIP implementation

Japanese Encephalitis: Global and Indian Epidemiology

Global Epidemiology

  • Endemic in 24 countries across WHO South-East Asia and Western Pacific
  • Affects over 3 billion people at risk
  • Annual clinical cases: ~100,000 (95% CI: 61,720–157,522)
  • Case fatality: 20-30% among symptomatic cases
  • Age group: Children <15 years (majority of cases)

Indian Epidemiology

  • High burden states: UP, Bihar, Assam, West Bengal
  • Annual cases: 1,000-3,000 reported (likely underreported)
  • Seasonal pattern: July-October (monsoon season)
  • Risk factors: Rice cultivation, pig farming, irrigation systems
  • NVBDCP sentinel surveillance in endemic districts

JE: Indian Context and Risk Factors

Vaccination Impact

  • Cases declined significantly since UIP introduction (2011)
  • Current status: Sporadic outbreaks in endemic areas
  • High-risk groups: Unvaccinated children in rural areas
  • Surveillance: IDSP Acute Encephalitis Syndrome (AES) monitoring

Transmission Dynamics

  • Rural epidemiology: Rice cultivation, pig farming
  • Monsoon correlation: Peak transmission July-October
  • Vector: Culex tritaeniorhynchus (rural mosquito)
  • Amplifying hosts: Domestic pigs, ardeid birds (herons, egrets)

JE Clinical Features and Timeline

Clinical progression timeline for both diseases

Clinical Presentation

  • Incubation: 4-14 days
  • Mild (99%): Fever, headache, myalgia
  • Severe (1 in 250): Encephalitis, seizures, paralysis
  • Indian context: Acute Encephalitis Syndrome (AES)
  • Complications: Coma, death, neurological deficits

Diagnosis in India

  • Clinical suspicion: Endemic areas, monsoon season
  • Laboratory: IgM ELISA on CSF (preferred)
  • RT-PCR: Early stages of infection
  • IDSP guidelines: Sample collection and testing
  • Differential: Other encephalitis syndromes

Chikungunya: Global and Indian Epidemiology

Global Epidemiology

  • 110+ countries affected since 1952
  • Major outbreaks in Africa, Asia, Americas, Europe
  • Annual burden: Millions affected during epidemics
  • Case fatality: <0.1% (rarely fatal)
  • Age groups: All ages, adults often more symptomatic

Indian Epidemiology

  • All states affected, major outbreaks every few years
  • 2006 epidemic: 1.3 million cases
  • Recent outbreaks: Delhi (2016-2017), Punjab, Haryana
  • Urban and rural transmission patterns
  • Vectors: Aedes aegypti (urban), Aedes albopictus (rural)

Clinical Features and Symptom Timeline

Disease progression and clinical phases comparison

JE Clinical Presentation

  • Incubation: 4-14 days
  • Mild phase: Fever, headache, myalgia (99% cases)
  • Severe phase: Encephalitis, seizures, paralysis (1 in 250)
  • Indian presentation: Acute Encephalitis Syndrome (AES)
  • Complications: Neurological deficits, death

Chikungunya Clinical Presentation

  • Incubation: 4-8 days (range 2-12 days)
  • Acute phase: High fever, severe joint pain, rash
  • Characteristic: "Chikungunya gait" due to joint pain
  • Chronic phase: Persistent arthritis (10-30% patients)
  • Complications: Neurological, cardiac, ocular (rare)

Conclusion and Key Takeaways

Japanese Encephalitis

  • Epidemiology: Rural, high fatality (20-30%), vaccine-preventable
  • Clinical: Neurological impact, intensive care needed
  • Prevention: UIP vaccination (95% effective), vector control
  • Indian program: Universal Immunization Programme
  • Surveillance: AES monitoring through IDSP

Chikungunya

  • Epidemiology: Urban/rural, chronic morbidity, vector control essential
  • Clinical: Rheumatic symptoms, outpatient management
  • Prevention: Source reduction (95% effective), personal protection
  • Indian program: NVBDCP integrated vector management
  • Surveillance: Mandatory reporting through IDSP

Future Directions and Challenges

Climate Change Impact

  • Vector expansion: New areas becoming suitable for transmission
  • Extended seasons: Longer transmission periods
  • Urban migration: Changing disease patterns
  • Adaptation needed: Enhanced surveillance and control measures

Research and Development

  • Chikungunya vaccines: Ixchiq, Valneva (limited availability)
  • Improved diagnostics: Rapid point-of-care tests
  • Vector control: New insecticides, biological controls
  • Digital surveillance: Real-time monitoring systems

Healthcare System Challenges

  • Rural healthcare: Limited access to diagnostics and treatment
  • Urban outbreaks: Overburdened healthcare facilities
  • Chronic care: Long-term management of sequelae
  • Health equity: Addressing disparities in disease burden

Policy and Implementation

  • Integrated approach: Combining JE vaccination with Chikungunya control
  • Community engagement: Essential for source reduction
  • Intersectoral coordination: Health, environment, urban planning
  • Monitoring and evaluation: Evidence-based policy decisions
